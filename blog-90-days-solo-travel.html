90 Days Away: What Solo Travel Taught Me About Growth, Loneliness, and Gratitude

After three months on the road, I’ve realized the real “destination” isn’t a place—it’s the mindset shift that happens when you’re forced to live outside your comfort zone.

Continuing on from my last update, I wanted to take a step back and write something more personal. This trip has been an amazing experience—full of new places, new faces, and moments I’ll remember for the rest of my life. But it’s also been challenging in ways I didn’t fully understand until I was living it.

Somewhere along the way, the trip stopped being just about seeing cool things. It became about learning how to handle the hard parts: missing home, navigating constantly, and figuring out how to be okay when it’s just you.

Key Takeaways

  • Solo travel pushes you to grow through constant problem-solving and independence.
  • Loneliness is a normal part of long-term travel—and learning to sit with it changes you.
  • Strangers can become some of the most meaningful parts of the journey.
  • If you get the opportunity to go, take it—you may never have the same window again.

The Best Part Has Been the People

I’ve met so many people on this trip, and honestly, that’s been the most surprising part. People have been incredibly open with me—welcoming me in, chatting with me, sharing stories, and making the world feel smaller.

One of my favorite moments was meeting a couple from Baton Rouge, Louisiana. We crossed paths on the mountain and they ended up buying me a steak lunch. It was simple, but it stuck with me because it was genuine kindness from people who didn’t owe me anything.

“When you’re on your own, kindness means more—because you don’t have anyone else to lean on.”

The Hard Part: Your Brain Never Gets to Turn Off

The reality of long-term travel is that you’re always thinking. It’s not just “vacation mode.” You’re constantly solving problems: figuring out where to go, how to get there, what’s open, what’s safe, what’s worth it.

Even simple things require effort. Finding a place to work. Making a meeting. Navigating a new town. Sometimes it feels like you’re living life with your brain permanently switched on.

And that pressure is where the growth happens

The flip side is that it forces you to build confidence. Every day you handle something new—something unfamiliar—and you realize you’re capable of more than you thought. It’s uncomfortable, but it’s also exciting.

Loneliness Is Part of the Deal (And That’s Okay)

There have definitely been low points. I’ve missed my friends, my family, and the comfort of normal life more than I expected. There’s something about being gone for 90 days that makes you feel the absence of home in a deeper way.

But I’ve also realized loneliness is a natural part of being away this long. Instead of trying to “fix it” all the time, I’ve been learning how to sit with it. To be okay with it. And even to enjoy the quiet moments.

  • Watching a sitcom at night and letting my brain rest
  • Calling family and catching up
  • Journaling when I don’t feel like talking
  • Learning bits of a language just for fun

Working East Coast Hours Abroad Has Been a Grind

One of the biggest adjustments has been working East Coast hours while traveling. It’s not impossible, but it adds another layer of discipline to everything. Some days it feels like I’m balancing two different worlds at once.

Still, I’ve made it work. I’ve adapted. And I’m proud of that—because it would have been easy to let the schedule beat me down.

A Reset on Life (And a New Kind of Gratitude)

If I had to sum up the biggest change, it’s that life feels simpler now. Not “easy”—but simpler. I’ve gotten clearer on what I actually need, what I value, and what I want my life to look like.

I feel happier. More content. Less judgmental. More patient. More appreciative. I’m not perfect, and I still have a lot to work on, but this experience has genuinely shifted my perspective.

Conclusion

When I look back on this trip, I don’t think the accomplishment is just the places I’ve seen. The accomplishment is making the most of the opportunity—taking it in stride, learning from the hard parts, and growing through it.

And if I had one thing to tell anyone thinking about doing something like this: do it. Take the chance. You might never get the same opportunity again—and even the difficult moments will become memories you’re proud of.

Frequently Asked Questions

What do you learn from 90 days of solo travel?

You learn independence, patience, and resilience. Long-term solo travel forces you to solve problems daily, build confidence, and get comfortable being alone while still staying open to meeting people.

How do you deal with loneliness while traveling alone?

The biggest shift is accepting that loneliness is normal. Building small routines—journaling, calling family, resting when needed, and doing simple things you enjoy—makes it manageable and even meaningful.

Is it hard to work remotely while traveling?

Yes, especially across time zones. The key is staying consistent with your schedule, planning ahead, and giving yourself grace when travel days make everything harder than expected.

What are common mistakes people make during long-term travel?

Trying to do too much, moving too fast, and expecting every day to feel magical. Long travel includes quiet days, frustrating logistics, and emotional dips—and that’s part of what makes it real.

Should you travel if you have the chance?

If you can, yes. You may never get the same window again. Even when it’s difficult, you’ll come home with a new perspective and a deeper appreciation for life.
